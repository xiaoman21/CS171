Content Is Getting Shorter

Across top influencers, brands, and media companies, average video length is trending down.

However, the quantity of videos is on an upward trajectory.

Shorter run times let viewers sample more content in less time--and keep scrolling.

The new default is fast, frequent, and highly visual, giving a taste of what the brand has to offer.

Zohran Mamdani: the candidate who started under 1% and won the whole race.

When Zohran Mamdani launched his mayoral campaign a year earlier, he was a little-known state assemblyman polling below 1%.

The race became a high-turnout, high-stakes referendum on affordability, inequality.

On Election Day, he won over 50% of the vote in a three-way race against former Governor Andrew Cuomo and Republican Curtis Sliwa, delivering New York City its first Muslim mayor.

Mamdani didn't just win young voters ‚Äì he ran on them.

Exit polls show 75% of young voters (18‚Äì29) backed Mamdani, making them his strongest age group by far.

He also won 66% of people voting for mayor for the first time, meaning he didn't just flip existing voters ‚Äì he reached new ones.

That follower gap isn't just cosmetic; it signals a massively larger direct channel into the everyday media diets of young New Yorkers.

His campaign used short-form video to:

  • Break down complex policy (housing, transit, childcare) into quick, sharable clips.
  • Showcase authenticity and relatability, making him a "credible messenger" for younger voters' anxieties about cost of living and inequality.
  • Reach new or low-propensity voters who historically don't show up in municipal elections.

Just as Trump's 2016 run demonstrated how social media can unlock new, previously disengaged voters, Mamdani's 2025 win shows how short-form platforms can power an outsider-style upset in local politics.

For parties, media, and brands, the lesson is clear:

If you want to shape mainstream political outcomes, you have to win the short-form attention market first.

Mamdani's <1% to mayor victory underscores that candidates who can speak fluently in short-form formats can connect more effectively with people in the digital era.
